\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

Code optimization is a fundamental area of computer science concerned with transforming programs to improve their execution time, memory usage, and energy consumption. At the compiler level, this involves selecting and parameterizing sequences of transformations such as loop tiling, interchange, fusion, vectorization, and parallelization. Traditionally, compilers rely on hand-crafted heuristics developed by domain experts, a process that is slow, fragile across architectures, and difficult to generalize to new program patterns. The core difficulty is the \textbf{phase ordering problem}: transformations interact in non-linear ways, and a locally beneficial decision can preclude a globally superior configuration~\cite{touati2014phase}. These challenges have motivated a growing interest in automated, data-driven approaches to compiler optimization.

Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a compelling alternative to manually designed heuristics. In RL, an agent learns to make sequential decisions by interacting with an environment and receiving a numerical reward signal that indicates the quality of its actions~\cite{sutton2018reinforcement}. Unlike supervised learning, RL does not require a dataset of correct answers, only the ability to evaluate outcomes empirically. This makes RL well suited to compiler optimization, where the optimal schedule for a given program and hardware target is not known in advance but can be measured through execution time. Deep reinforcement learning, which employs neural networks as function approximators, has demonstrated the ability to learn sophisticated strategies in domains ranging from game playing to chip design, and is now being explored as a path toward adaptive, architecture-aware compiler scheduling.

The Multi-Level Intermediate Representation (MLIR), introduced by Lattner et al.~\cite{lattner2021mlir}, is a compiler infrastructure within the LLVM ecosystem that supports multiple dialects at different levels of abstraction within a single representation. MLIR's Linalg dialect explicitly encodes the structure of linear algebra operations, while the Transform dialect enables the programmatic application and composition of transformation schedules. Together with MLIR's Python bindings, these features provide a natural platform for building RL-based optimization systems that can observe, transform, and evaluate code within a unified pipeline.

The application of RL to MLIR-based optimization was pioneered by Bendib et al.~\cite{bendib2024reinforcement}, who developed an RL environment for optimizing individual Linalg operations. Tirichine et al.~\cite{tirichine2025reinforcement} subsequently extended this environment to support full neural network computation graphs, introducing an abstract syntax tree (AST) based state representation with a recursive LSTM encoder and a Fusion action. Their work demonstrated the feasibility of RL-based full-program optimization in MLIR, but also revealed several limitations: the agent's observation was hardware-agnostic, the reward function provided feedback only at episode termination, the sequential LSTM encoder struggled with long-range loop dependencies, and the action space lacked transformations such as padding, packing, and unrolling. The present thesis addresses each of these limitations. We introduce a \textbf{hardware-aware observation space} that encodes the target processor's cache hierarchy, core count, and SIMD width directly into the agent's input. We design a \textbf{shaped reward function} that provides dense intermediate feedback at every transformation step using static code analysis. We replace the recursive LSTM with a \textbf{transformer-based loop nest encoder} capable of directly attending to relationships between any pair of loop levels. We expand the action space with \textbf{pad, pack, and unroll transformations} for finer control over data layout and instruction-level parallelism. These improvements are evaluated both individually and in combination to measure their contribution to optimization performance.

The remainder of this manuscript is organized as follows. Chapter~1 provides background on compiler code optimization, loop nest transformations, and the MLIR infrastructure. Chapter~2 introduces the foundations of reinforcement learning, from Markov decision processes through policy gradient methods and Proximal Policy Optimization. Chapter~3 surveys related work on applying RL to compiler optimization and provides a comparative synthesis. Chapter~4 presents the design and implementation of our system, detailing the observation space, action space, reward function, and policy architecture. Chapter~5 describes the experimental methodology and results. A general conclusion summarizes our findings and outlines directions for future research.

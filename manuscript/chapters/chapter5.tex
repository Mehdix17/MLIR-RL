\section{Introduction}

The previous chapter described the design of our proposed RL auto-scheduler. This chapter empirically evaluates the proposed \gls{rl} auto-scheduler to quantify the impact of its architectural enhancements: hardware-aware observations, shaped rewards, and the transformer-based encoder. We benchmark the system against standard MLIR heuristics and the prior MLIR-RL agent~\cite{tirichine2025reinforcement}, and validate individual components via rigorous ablation studies.

The chapter is organized as follows: Section~5.2 details the experimental setup. Section~5.3 presents the overall system performance, followed by ablation studies in Section~5.4. Section~5.5 discusses the findings, and Section~5.6 concludes the chapter.

By training the RL agent on Dalma and evaluating it across Jubail and Bergamo, we comprehensively test the agent's capability to adapt dynamically to wildly different core counts and cache topographies.

The software stack consists of the following components and configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Python~3.11 and PyTorch~2.0~\cite{paszke2019pytorch}.
    \item MLIR built from the LLVM~19.x release branch with \gls{mlir} Python bindings.
    \item \gls{mlir} benchmarks compiled via progressive lowering (Linalg to Affine to \gls{scf} to Vector to \gls{llvm}) and \gls{jit}-compiled with optimization level \texttt{-O3} using the MLIR ExecutionEngine.
    \item Execution time measurements are performed on zero-initialized buffers to eliminate data-dependent variability, with a warm-up invocation preceding each timed invocation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary evaluation metric is the execution-time speedup, defined as the ratio of the unoptimized MLIR-Base execution time to the optimized execution time:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Speedup} = \frac{T_\text{MLIR-Base}}{T_\text{optimized}}
\end{equation}

A speedup greater than $1.0$ indicates an improvement over the standard compiler heuristics. For aggregated comparisons across multiple benchmarks, we report the geometric mean of the per-benchmark speedups, which correctly balances multiplicative ratio values:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Geometric Mean Speedup} = \left( \prod_{i=1}^{n} \text{Speedup}_i \right)^{1/n}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Impact of Shaped Rewards (No-Reward-Shaping)}

The Proposed System employs a multi-objective shaped reward based on static efficiency metrics (arithmetic intensity, vectorizability, parallel-loop ratio) applied at each step, alongside the sparse terminal speedup reward. The \textbf{No-Reward-Shaping} variant falls back to using only the terminal execution speedup as its feedback signal.

Beyond final execution speedup, the impact of reward shaping is most evident in the training dynamics. The dense intermediate feedback drastically reduces the variance of policy updates and accelerates convergence.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Contribution of Individual Components}
The ablation studies solidify the rationale behind the proposed architectural changes. The hardware-aware features successfully untether the learned policies from the training platform's specific architectural quirks. Dense, shaped rewards solve the credit assignment problem during the fragile early stages of training, leading to faster and more reliable convergence. Finally, the Transformer encoder natively absorbs the complex topological dependencies in loop-nests, decisively outperforming the legacy LSTM implementations on intricate neural network sub-graphs involving four or more cascading loops. 

\subsection{Limitations}
Despite significant advances, the proposed auto-scheduler retains some limitations. The system relies entirely on predefined heuristic handlers and rigid transformation definitions. Furthermore, JIT-compiling every candidate sequence within the reinforcement learning loop remains a massive computational bottleneck, preventing the agent from being deployed for entirely on-the-fly, real-time optimization in production compilers.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}
Our approach marks a substantial departure from the prior MLIR-RL agent and foundational systems like Halide and Tiramisu. While PolyGym tackled polyhedral transformations via MDPs, it remained confined to highly regular static domains. The proposed system effectively bridges these domains into unstructured deep learning ecosystems by actively managing dynamic configurations, injecting hardware realities directly into the policy state, and fortifying the structural analysis via modern Transformer layouts.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter presented a rigorous, ablation-focused evaluation of our proposed \gls{rl} auto-scheduler for the MLIR compiler framework. By establishing strong baselines against both the standard \gls{mlir} compilation steps (MLIR-Base) and the prior state-of-the-art \gls{rl} implementation (MLIR-RL), we demonstrated that the integrated system comfortably exceeds previous benchmarks in both simple linear algebra domains and full, real-world neural network graph executions.

Through isolated ablation variants, we quantified the exact utility of each theoretical contribution: the hardware-aware observation, the static shaped rewards, and the Transformer-based encoder. The findings conclusively show that merging these concepts enables deep reinforcement learning to scale reliably to complex, dynamic, and hardware-agnostic compiler optimizations. The final chapter will summarize the scope of this project and outline prominent avenues for future research.

\chapter{Experiments and Results}
\label{ch:experiments-results}

\section{Introduction}

The previous chapter described the design of our proposed \gls{rl} auto-scheduler. This chapter empirically evaluates the system to measure the impact of its three architectural enhancements: hardware-aware observations, shaped rewards, and the transformer-based encoder. We first conduct a controlled ablation study that trains four agent variants---each disabling a single component---to isolate the contribution of each enhancement. The best-performing variant is then selected for head-to-head comparisons against two baselines: the standard \gls{mlir} heuristics (\gls{mlir}-Base) and the prior \gls{mlir}-\gls{rl} agent~\cite{tirichine2025reinforcement}.

The agent is trained on Bergamo (256-core AMD EPYC 9754) and evaluated on all three clusters. This three-cluster setup tests the agent's ability to adapt its schedules across different core counts, cache topologies, and \gls{cpu} architectures.

The software stack consists of the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Python~3.11 and PyTorch~2.0~\cite{paszke2019pytorch}.
    \item \gls{mlir} built from \gls{llvm}~19.x with \gls{mlir} Python bindings.
    \item Benchmarks are compiled via progressive lowering (Linalg to Affine to \gls{scf} to Vector to \gls{llvm}) and \gls{jit}-compiled with \texttt{-O3} using the \gls{mlir} ExecutionEngine.
    \item Execution times are measured on zero-initialized buffers to eliminate data-dependent variability, with a warm-up invocation preceding each timed invocation.
\end{itemize}

Each training iteration collects trajectories under the current policy, followed by a \gls{ppo} update using the Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2014adam}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Each optimized benchmark is executed once on the target hardware. The primary evaluation metric is the execution-time speedup, defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Speedup} = \frac{T_\text{\gls{mlir}-Base}}{T_\text{optimized}}
\end{equation}

$T_\text{\gls{mlir}-Base}$ is the execution time of the standard \gls{mlir} compilation pipeline with \texttt{-O3} but without any \gls{rl}-guided transformations. A speedup above $1.0$ indicates improvement over the standard compiler.

For aggregated comparisons across multiple benchmarks, we report the geometric mean of per-benchmark speedups, the standard metric for normalized ratios that resists skew from outliers:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Geometric Mean Speedup} = \left( \prod_{i=1}^{n} \text{Speedup}_i \right)^{1/n}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Datasets}

We use two evaluation sets : \\ \\
\textbf{Single-operation set:} contains 819 benchmarks covering four operation types: matrix multiplication (232 benchmarks), 2D convolution (71 benchmarks), element-wise ReLU operations (106 benchmarks), and pooling (315 benchmarks). These benchmarks are extracted from 18 neural network architectures spanning vision, language, and graph models.

\textbf{Full-model set:} contains 918 operation blocks extracted from the same 18 architectures. Blocks are windows of 5 consecutive operations extracted with a stride of 3. Each model contributes between 11 and 150 blocks. All blocks are drawn from the training set, as blocks were placed entirely in training to prevent data leakage through overlapping extraction windows (Section~4.2).

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation-multi-hw}

To isolate the contribution of each architectural enhancement, we trained four agent variants under identical PPO hyperparameters, differing by exactly one component:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Full Agent:} all three novelties enabled: hardware-aware observation, shaped reward (arithmetic intensity, vectorizability, parallel-loop ratio), and Transformer encoder.
    \item \textbf{No-Reward-Shaping Agent:} hardware-aware observation and Transformer encoder, relying solely on the terminal speedup signal without intermediate shaped rewards.
    \item \textbf{No-Transformer Agent:} hardware-aware observation and shaped reward, but using an \gls{lstm} encoder instead of the Transformer.
    \item \textbf{No-Hardware-Features Agent:} shaped reward and Transformer encoder, but with all hardware features set to zero (hardware-agnostic).
\end{enumerate}

All 4 variants were trained on the Bergamo cluster for 10{,}000 iterations and evaluated on the full-model set. \\
Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-study} presents per-model speedups across 18 model families for all four variants.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/ablation_study.png}
    \caption{Per-model ablation study comparing all four agent variants across 18 model families at checkpoint 1{,}100.}
    \label{fig:ablation-study}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-study} reveals a clear ranking. The No-Reward-Shaping agent achieves the highest overall speedups, outperforming the Full Agent on the majority of models. On Albert, the No-Reward-Shaping agent reaches 7.99x while the Full Agent trails at 3.85x. On BART, the gap is 6.72x versus 3.33x. This result indicates that the static heuristics used for shaped rewards---arithmetic intensity, vectorizability, and parallel-loop ratio---are imperfect proxies for actual execution speedup. The agent learns to maximize these heuristics rather than the true objective, producing suboptimal schedules. Removing shaped reward entirely and relying solely on the terminal speedup signal produces a more robust policy.

The No-Transformer agent (LSTM encoder) underperforms the Full Agent on most models, confirming that the Transformer encoder provides a consistent advantage. The gap is most visible on deep loop nests such as those in T5 and Llama~3.2~1B, where long-range dependencies between outer tiling loops and inner vectorization targets matter. For shallow nests common in simpler operations, the two encoders perform comparably. The Transformer's self-attention mechanism processes all loop levels simultaneously, preserving hierarchical relationships that the \gls{lstm}'s sequential encoding can degrade.

The No-Hardware-Features agent trails on most models, particularly on Albert and BART, where cache-sensitive tiling decisions are critical. Without hardware context, the agent cannot condition its tile size choices on the available L1/L2/L3 cache sizes, leading to suboptimal cache utilization.

The No-Reward-Shaping agent emerges as the best-performing variant and is selected for all subsequent experiments (Sections~5.4 and~5.5). We refer to this variant as \textbf{Our Agent} in the remainder of this chapter.

\subsection{Hardware-Aware Observation: Multi-Platform Analysis}

To test whether hardware features enable cross-platform generalization, we evaluate the impact of hardware-awareness by comparing two configurations of the No-Reward-Shaping agent: one receiving the full 7-element hardware vector (cache sizes, core counts, \gls{simd} width, clock speed), and one receiving a zero-filled hardware vector. Both share the same trained model weights. We evaluate on three clusters at checkpoint 1{,}100. Bergamo is the training cluster (AMD EPYC 9754, 256 cores). Dalma uses a fundamentally different architecture (Intel Xeon E5-2680 v4, 28 cores). Jubail shares the AMD EPYC lineage but has half the cores (EPYC 7742, 128 cores). This cross-platform setup tests whether hardware features enable zero-shot transfer.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/graph2_bergamo.png}
    \caption[Multi-hardware evaluation on Bergamo]{Multi-hardware evaluation on Bergamo. Top: geometric mean speedup by operation type. Bottom: by model family.}
    \label{fig:multi-hw-bergamo}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:multi-hw-bergamo} shows results on the training cluster. The hardware-aware variant outperforms the no-hardware variant in almost all categories. The gap is largest on convolution (6.20x vs 1.61x), pooling (3.00x vs 1.18x), and models such as Albert, BART, and T5, where the hardware-aware variant achieves 4--10x speedups while the no-hardware variant achieves 2--5x. On the training hardware, hardware features provide critical context about cache sizes and \gls{simd} width that directly inform tile size selection and vectorization decisions.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/graph2_jubail.png}
    \caption{Multi-hardware evaluation on Jubail.}
    \label{fig:multi-hw-jubail}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:multi-hw-jubail} shows Jubail results. The hardware-aware and no-hardware variants perform nearly identically across all categories. This similarity indicates that the scheduling strategies learned on Bergamo transfer well to other AMD EPYC processors without needing hardware-specific adjustments. The architectural similarity between Zen 4 (Bergamo) and Zen 2 (Jubail) means the optimal tile sizes and parallelization degrees remain effective.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/graph2_dalma.png}
    \caption{Multi-hardware evaluation on Dalma.}
    \label{fig:multi-hw-dalma}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:multi-hw-dalma} shows Dalma results. Despite the architectural gap (AMD to Intel), the hardware-aware variant still holds advantages on several categories. Convolution drops to 1.47x vs 1.71x---the no-hardware variant slightly edges ahead here. Matrix multiplication is nearly identical at 2.36x vs 2.30x. On the model side, Albert achieves 5.68x vs 3.07x, showing the hardware-aware variant's persistent advantage on transformer attention blocks.

The key finding across all three clusters is that hardware-aware features provide strong benefits on the training platform and maintain a smaller but consistent advantage when transferred to dissimilar hardware. The benefits are most pronounced on the training cluster because the policy learns to exploit Bergamo-specific hardware characteristics during training. On the architecturally similar Jubail, the two variants converge.

In summary, the ablation study establishes three conclusions. First, the No-Reward-Shaping agent---relying solely on the terminal speedup signal---outperforms all shaped-reward variants, and is selected as the best configuration. Second, the Transformer encoder provides a consistent advantage over the \gls{lstm} encoder, especially on deep loop nests. Third, hardware-aware features deliver their largest benefit on the training platform and degrade gracefully on dissimilar hardware, never hurting performance.

\section{Overall Performance}
\label{sec:overall-performance}

\subsection{Single-Operation Speedup}

This experiment compares our agent---the No-Reward-Shaping variant identified as the best configuration by the ablation study (Section~5.3)---against the prior \gls{mlir}-\gls{rl} agent on 298 single-operation benchmarks grouped by operation type. Both agents are trained on the Bergamo cluster for 10{,}000 iterations and evaluated on a held-out test set of unseen benchmarks. Our agent uses hardware-aware observation and the Transformer encoder, relying solely on the terminal speedup signal. The prior agent uses none of these enhancements.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/graph1_performance.png}
    \caption{Single-operation geometric mean speedups: Prior Agent vs Our Agent}
    \label{fig:overall-performance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:overall-performance} shows the geometric mean speedup per operation type for both agents. Our agent outperforms the prior agent by a large margin on convolution (6.20x vs 2.63x), ReLU (3.16x vs 2.20x), and pooling (3.12x vs 2.20x). On matrix multiplication, the prior agent achieves a higher geometric mean (10.62x vs 2.00x). This result suggests that the prior agent specializes effectively on dense linear algebra, where simple tiling and parallelization strategies are sufficient. Our agent achieves more balanced performance across all operation types, improving the weakest categories (ReLU and pooling) substantially.

Our agent's ability to handle element-wise operations and pooling---where transformation choices are less obvious than for matrix multiplication---demonstrates the benefit of the Transformer encoder, which captures hierarchical loop dependencies that simpler models miss. The hardware-aware features further enable the agent to select tile sizes and parallelization strategies appropriate for the Bergamo hardware.

\section{Full Neural Network Models}
\label{sec:full-models}

\subsection{End-to-End Model Speedup}

This experiment evaluates both agents on the full-model evaluation set: 918 operation blocks drawn from the training set (all extracted blocks were placed in training to prevent data leakage; Section~4.2). For each model, we sum the baseline execution times of all its blocks and the optimized execution times produced by each agent. The total model speedup is the ratio of these two sums:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Total Model Speedup} = \frac{\sum_{b \in \text{model}} T_\text{Base}(b)}{\sum_{b \in \text{model}} T_\text{Optimized}(b)}
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{ressources/chapter5/graph3_fullmodel.png}
    \caption{Total model speedup on 18 full neural network architectures. The prior agent is shown only for models where it completes successfully. Bars are grouped by architecture.}
    \label{fig:full-models}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:full-models} reports per-model speedups. Our agent achieves a speedup above 1.0x on 17 out of 18 models. The largest gains appear on T5 (10.58x), Albert (8.00x), BART (6.72x), Llama~3.2~1B (9.62x), and ResNet-50 (4.98x). These models contain many batch matrix multiplications and structured convolutions that benefit strongly from tiling and parallelization.

The prior agent fails to support several architectures. It cannot produce valid schedules for bart, gat, gin, GPT-2, Whisper, YOLO, ViT, and T5, where its search strategy produces transformation sequences that cause \gls{mlir} compilation failures. Our agent avoids these failures through its robust training design, which penalizes transformation sequences that result in compilation or execution errors.

MobileNetV3-Small shows a notable result: the prior agent scores 2.50x while our agent scores 1.76x. This model is dominated by depthwise convolutions, a pattern where aggressive tiling and parallelization---the prior agent's strength---happen to work well without stability concerns. Our agent's more conservative strategy sacrifices some speedup on this model in exchange for robustness on the other seventeen.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This chapter evaluated our proposed \gls{rl} auto-scheduler through systematic experiments and ablation studies. The ablation study established that the agent without reward shaping consistently outperforms the full shaped-reward agent, achieving the highest speedups across model families. The Transformer encoder provides a consistent advantage over the \gls{lstm} encoder, particularly on deep loop nests where self-attention captures long-range dependencies between outer tiling loops and inner vectorization targets. Hardware-aware features benefit the agent on the training platform and degrade gracefully on dissimilar hardware, never hurting performance.

Compared against baselines, our agent---the No-Reward-Shaping variant with hardware-aware observation and Transformer encoder---achieves substantial speedups on single operations and successfully optimizes 17 out of 18 full neural network architectures. The largest gains appear on transformer-based models (T5, Albert, BART, Llama~3.2~1B), where tiling and parallelization of attention operations yield large speedups.

Several limitations define the scope of this work. Training requires \gls{jit}-compiling every candidate transformation sequence, making each iteration expensive; a single training run of 10{,}000 iterations takes approximately 70 hours on a 28-core node. The static heuristics used for reward shaping (arithmetic intensity, vectorizability ratio) proved to be unreliable proxies for execution speedup on many operation types, motivating the removal of shaped rewards entirely. The evaluation is limited to \gls{cpu} targets; extending to \gls{gpu} and accelerator backends would require different transformation priorities and hardware features. Finally, the action space is fixed to five transformation types (tiling, parallelization, fusion, interchange, vectorization); additional transformations such as loop unrolling, padding, and packing remain unexplored.

The failure of shaped rewards to improve performance teaches an important lesson: intermediate reward signals must be carefully calibrated to avoid dominating the terminal objective. When the proxy heuristics do not correlate with execution speedup, they mislead the policy rather than guide it. The next chapter concludes the thesis by summarizing these findings and outlining directions for future research.

# Manuscript Writing Comments

---

## Text Formatting / Spelling / Grammar

- Use spelling and grammar checking tools such as [Grammarly](http://grammarly.com).
- Respect typography rules appropriate for English (similar rules exist for [French](http://j.poitou.free.fr/pro/html/typ/resume.html) as a reference). Find the English-specific rules and follow them consistently.
- Be consistent in the capitalization of words in titles and subtitles. Follow a single rule across all headings. See [capitalization rules for titles/headings](https://www.scribbr.com/academic-writing/capitalization-titles-headings/). The specific rule chosen does not matter as long as it is applied consistently.
- Do not add a colon (`:`) at the end of a title or subtitle. The heading formatting is sufficient.

---

## Introduction

- The introduction, conclusion, and table of contents are the three most important sections of your manuscript. Most readers will read these to decide whether to read the full manuscript. Make these three sections as polished as possible.
- A well-structured introduction should:
  - Provide the general context of the problem.
  - Explain why the problem is important and challenging.
  - Clearly state the problem being solved.
  - Optionally, provide a high-level idea of the solution.
- The table of contents should have a clear flow of ideas so that readers can decide what to read just by looking at it.

---

## Bibliography

- Do not cite blogs, websites, or Wikipedia. Use research papers, books, and reports instead. Websites are unreliable and impermanent compared to books and academic publications.
- Every method or model mentioned in the manuscript must have a reference. For example, if you mention Tiramisu or Halide, cite the papers that introduced them.
- Every bibliographical entry must be complete:
  - Do not use "et al." in the bibliography. List the full names of all authors.
  - Include: full author list, title, and conference/journal name (for papers) or publisher name (for books).
  - Year of publication and other metadata are optional but recommended.

---

## Structure

- Chapters should be balanced in length (similar number of pages). Avoid having one chapter at 9 pages and another at 24. Chapters should ideally be 10 or more pages long, and no chapter should fall below 7 pages.
- At the end of your state-of-the-art chapter:
  - Add a comparison table (synthesis) of existing methods.
  - Add a detailed section criticizing the limitations and advantages of state-of-the-art methods. This section should be substantial (not just one or two pages) and will naturally lead into your contribution.
- Each chapter must have its own introduction and conclusion.
- Chapters should be linked to each other. At the end of each chapter, include a brief transition that explains how it connects to the next chapter.

---

## General

- Read your manuscript multiple times carefully. Each reading will reveal new spelling and grammatical errors.
- Never use a term without first introducing it. Every term should be defined before use.
- Consider adding a section that lists all abbreviations used. LaTeX can generate this automatically.
- Avoid subjective terms in a scientific document. Do not say something is "big," "good," or a "big improvement." These are subjective and have no place in scientific writing.
- Use automatic figure numbering and reference management (via Word or LaTeX). Manual numbering is error-prone: any reordering, addition, or removal of figures will require manually updating all references throughout the manuscript.
- When citing a paper, do not write "In [5] the system uses...". Instead, write "In Tiramisu [5], the authors use..." or "Baghdadi et al. [5] use...".
- Write for clarity and accessibility. Your goal is to produce a document that students can understand easily. Explain things step by step, introduce every concept intuitively before going into low-level details, and explain every term in every formula. Think of yourself as a teacher guiding students through your work.
- Whenever you write anything (an outline, section, chapter, etc.), use LLMs to get feedback. If the feedback is actionable, implement it. LLMs are effective reviewers.
